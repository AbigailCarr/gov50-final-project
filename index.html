My Research Question On average, do AI/AN populations have higher Covid rates in Arizona counties? Looking specifically at Navajo, Apache, and Yavapai counties (the counties which have the largest AI/AN populations) are the Covid rates higher than white people? How do the rates differ?

My theory: I would assume that Covid rates will be higher generally for AI/AN individuals.Currently in the United States, there are 324 federally recognized American Indian reservations in the US. Interestingly, only 13% of these AI/AN people live on historic reservations. While the United States is obligated to provide federal health care to the AI/AN people, health outcomes are “adversely impacted by wholly inadequate access to comprehensive health services” (American Bar Association). The Indian Health Services is notably underfunded by Congress, characterized by significant resource constraints (National Congress for Native Americans). Currently, AI/AN people suffer from higher rates of diabetes and related illness, heart disease, and substance abuse than any other racial group in the United States (Office of Minority Health). Due to the evident health crisis among AI/AN people, I would hypothesize that Covid rates are higher for AI/AN people — either because of the health crisis and lack of resources these people generally experience, but also because of a potential failure to report the cases properly by the individual county or state governments.

My Data Resources: Data sources consisted of two primary data sets: The first is Broadstreet Health Race and Ethnicity Covid Data January 2021, consisting of Covid data from January 2021 across the United States. This is organized by county/race/day — each observation is the cumulative total of these three variables. The second date set is the Census data regarding Arizona counties and the reported population estimates and Covid cases by race. This data sets presents raw estimates for each county.

Dependent Variable and the Independent Variable: The key dependent variable is cumulative cases per 100k people. After joining the data sets, we divided the number of cumulative cases by the number of people in that population/racial group and multiplied by 100K. This tells us the average number of people in that group who would have cumulatively had Covid if the group consisted of 100K. Independent variables are race. This is categorical. In our regression, we include a categorical variable for county and also include a quantitative viable days since the start of 2021.

# https://github.com/BroadStreet-Health/Race-and-Ethnicity-Data

covid_new <- read_csv("~/Downloads/covid_data_jan21.csv")

# Separating county into two columns to get county name and state name separate
# Grouping by county, state, and category first so that we can calculate difference
# in daily reporting for those groups separate from one another
# We are creating a variable for the last reported date & its cases so that we can calculate difference
# over time
# Difference over time is the difference in cases from the last report divided by the difference in days from
# the last report

covid_new_w_state <- covid_new |>
  mutate(cumul_cases = `0`) |>
  separate(col = county_names, into = c("county", "state"), sep = ', ',
           extra = 'drop',
           fill = 'warn') %>%
  group_by(county, state, categories) |>
  mutate(last_date_reported = lag(dates),
         last_cases_reported = lag(cumul_cases)) |>
  ungroup() |>
  mutate(dif_days = as.numeric(dates - last_date_reported),
         dif_cases = cumul_cases - last_cases_reported,
         daily_change = dif_cases / dif_days) |>
  arrange(county, state, categories)

Cleaning the Data - not every county had a report for every single day, we wanted to find the average change over that time period. I used the lag versions of both date variable and the cumulative case variables to explain the previous report, then found the difference between the last report and the present. For example, our date is January 10 and our last reported date was Jan 5. It is a difference of 5 days, and similarly, finding perhaps a difference of 100 cases, and then calculating a daily change by dividing difference in cases and difference in days.

For each of the three counties, we filtered for Arizona and plotted the differences in cases over the dates over time. It shows how prevalent Covid cases were at that time, basically on specific days how many new cases were there. Each chart shows how many new cases per day there were in each county for AI/AN individuals and other races. There are some days with negative values for unknowns, which means officials must have learned what the unknowns truly were and reclassified them into the correct categories. Looking at the three counties, in Apache county we see reclassification to AI/AN, particularly around January 7-8. We see a spike in AI/AN cases and a dip in unknown cases when these cases are reclassified. However, in Yavapai County, it appears the unclassified cases are generally white. It may be the case that in Yavapai there are more white people — it’s not necessarily indicative that the reporting error affects the races differently or if this is just population differences. Because it is uncertain whether these reclassification spikes are due to errors in reporting or just due to a county population being heavily AI/AN or white, the next step of my project looks at joining this data with census estimates in the given counties.

AZ_covid_w_dems <- inner_join(AZ_covid, AZ_county_dems, by = c("county" = "CTYNAME")) |>
  mutate(race_population = case_when(
    categories == "american_indian_alaska_native"  ~ IA_MALE + IA_FEMALE,
    categories == "asian" ~ AA_MALE + AA_FEMALE,
    categories == "black_african_american" ~ BA_MALE + BA_FEMALE,
    categories == "cumulative_cases" ~ TOT_POP,
    categories == "hispanic_(all_races)" ~ H_MALE + H_FEMALE,
    categories == "non-hispanic" ~ NH_MALE + NH_FEMALE,
    categories == "other"  ~ TOT_POP,
    categories == "unknown" ~ TOT_POP,
    categories == "white" ~ WA_MALE + WA_FEMALE,
    categories == "2+_races" ~ TOM_MALE + TOM_FEMALE,
    categories == "native_hawaiian_pacific_islander" ~ NA_MALE + NA_FEMALE,
    categories ==  "not_specified" ~ TOT_POP
  )) |>
  mutate(race = categories) |>
  select(county, state, dates, race, cumul_cases, dif_days, dif_cases, daily_change, race_population) |>
  mutate(cumul_cases_pc = 100000 * cumul_cases / race_population,
         dif_cases_pc = 100000 * dif_cases / race_population,
         daily_change_pc = 100000 * daily_change / race_population)

Next, the summary of the main dependent variable is, at the end of January in 2021, the distribution of cumulative cases per 100K at the county level in Arizona. Each distribution in the chart shows the cumulative case rates for each county by race group. There is a much greater variance at the county level compared to white people for AI/AN. The mean level is 2500 cases higher per 100K than the white population. Because there is higher variance in AI/AN populations, I specifically picked the three counties with higher AI/AN populations.

Furthermore, for the regression, I calculated the number of days for the report to the start of 2021. This put time as a number instead of a categorical variable. By making it a numerical variable, we are effectively forcing the slope to increase over time. Every additional day in 2021, how much would we expect AI/AN cases to increase? Our other two variables, race and county, are categorical.

Note: For coding, since AI/AN was the baseline, I only needed to intercept and the coefficient for days. Looking at white people (since it was not the reference) I needed to look at the coefficient on white and the coefficient for days. Similarly, not Apache county but Navajo county, since Apache was my reference but Navajo was not, I had to add the Navajo county coefficient and the coefficient days. The outcomes for this work tells us the regression equation for that race in that county over time.

As for the regression, we see that “racewhite” compared to AI/AN on average shows that white populations at the county level will see 3,370 less Covid cases per 100K than AI/AN people, holding the time and county constant. We are not looking across counties or from the start to the end of the month — rather, one specific county on one specific day. The p-value is 0, meaning it’s statistically significant, so we can be fairly sure that on average white populations had lower Covid rates than AI/AN populations in these three counties in Arizona.
